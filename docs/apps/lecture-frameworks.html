Chapter 1 · Method selection

Introduction to Environmental Geophysics

Build the central habit of the course: translate a site question into a detectable physical-property contrast, then design a survey that can actually sample it.

Deck basis: Environmental Geophysics—Introduction & Course Overview.
1 · Why image?Coverage, risk reduction, and the limits of intrusive sampling.
2 · What is measured?Fields, contrasts, anomalies, active versus passive methods.
3 · Can we detect it?Target size, depth, spacing, noise, logistics, and ground truth.
0–8 min · HookChoose between sparse pits and a spatial survey for a buried hazard.
8–24 min · Physics mapMatch density, susceptibility, velocity, resistivity, and permittivity to methods.
24–38 min · DesignWork through sampling, aliasing, noise, positioning, and constraints.
38–47 min · Mission planTeams design a survey for a steel underground tank near cultural noise.
47–50 min · ExitName the measured property, expected signal, and strongest ambiguity.

Board / slide essentials

  • Question → target → contrast → method → geometry → data → model → interpretation.
  • Resolution, depth, coverage, and signal-to-noise trade-offs.
  • Spatial sampling smaller than the feature scale; no universal depth rule.

Misconception to surface

Geophysics does not detect “groundwater” or “a drum” directly. It detects a contrast whose geological cause is non-unique.

Undergraduate Core
Defend one primary method and station spacing for a stated target.
Graduate Extension
Frame survey design as expected information gain under noise, cost, and prior uncertainty.

Chapter 2 · Density contrast

Gravity Methods

Move from Newtonian attraction and density contrast to field reduction, residual anomalies, and deliberately limited source-depth estimates.

Deck basis: Gravity Methods—Physical Basis, Measurements, Corrections, and Interpretation.
1 · SignalNewton's law, acceleration units, density contrast, scale of a mGal.
2 · ReductionBase loop, tides, latitude, free air, Bouguer slab, and terrain.
3 · InterpretationRegional/residual separation, simple sources, half-width, non-uniqueness.
0–7 min · HookCan missing rock mass beneath a road be measured from the surface?
7–20 min · PhysicsRelate anomaly sign and width to density contrast and depth.
20–35 min · CorrectionsConstruct one sign-consistent reduction table from a base loop.
35–46 min · InterpretMeasure half amplitude and test an ideal-sphere depth estimate.
46–50 min · ExitSeparate “gravity low” from “confirmed cavity.”

Equation / visual set

  • \(g=GM/r^2\), \(\Delta g\) from a density contrast.
  • Relative Bouguer anomaly with every correction sign documented.
  • Sphere only: \(z\approx1.305x_{1/2}\).

Misconception to surface

Terrain correction is positive under the standard convention, and a half-width relation is not transferable to an incompletely defined cylinder or arbitrary cavity.

Undergraduate Core
Reduce the revised cavity dataset and explain the negative anomaly.
Graduate Extension
Propagate density, terrain, regional-removal, and geometry uncertainty into the depth claim.

Chapter 3 · Magnetization

Magnetic Methods

Connect the geomagnetic field, induced and remanent magnetization, instrumentation, processing, and the geometry-dependent shape of anomalies.

Deck basis: Geomagnetic Methods—Earth's Field, Instruments, Interpretation, and Processing.
1 · Field & sourcesF, inclination, declination, susceptibility, remanence.
2 · Measure & cleanSensor types, base station, gradiometry, IGRF, leveling.
3 · Transform & inferContinuation, RTP limits, Peters-style reconnaissance, modeling.
0–8 min · HookWhy can a source and its anomaly peak be horizontally displaced?
8–23 min · PhysicsCompare induced and remanent vectors under different field inclinations.
23–35 min · WorkflowInterpolate a base record and remove a stated regional field.
35–46 min · Hazard profileLocate a ferrous target while resisting false depth precision.
46–50 min · ExitName one condition that invalidates RTP or a slope rule.

Data literacy

  • Total field (nT) versus a sensor difference (nT) versus a calibrated gradient (nT/m).
  • Diurnal correction relative to a common base reference.
  • Peak location depends on field direction, source geometry, and remanence.

Misconception to surface

A high–low pair is not a unique cylinder signature, and a zero crossing is not automatically the exact excavation point.

Undergraduate Core
Produce corrected total-field and two-sensor-difference profiles with units.
Graduate Extension
Test magnetization-direction and geometry sensitivity before applying RTP or depth estimators.

Chapter 4 · Elastic waves

Seismic Methods

Link elastic properties to P- and S-wave behavior, then use travel-time slopes and intercepts without hiding the assumptions behind refraction.

Deck basis: Seismic Exploration—Elasticity, Refraction, Reflection, and Environmental Applications.
1 · Wave physicsStress, strain, moduli, P/S waves, impedance.
2 · Travel timesDirect, reflected, critically refracted waves and first-break picks.
3 · Models & limitsIntercept time, reciprocity, hidden layers, tomography, uncertainty.
0–8 min · HookInterpret three straight branches on a T–X plot.
8–22 min · PhysicsDerive velocity from slope and reflection polarity from impedance.
22–38 min · CalculationFit the 500/1500/3000 m/s synthetic branches and intercepts.
38–47 min · Failure caseDiagnose a low-velocity aquitard invisible to head waves.
47–50 min · ExitState one acquisition check that could expose lateral variation.

Equation / visual set

  • \(V_P=\sqrt{(K+4G/3)/\rho}\), \(V_S=\sqrt{G/\rho}\).
  • T–X slope in s/m; velocity is its reciprocal.
  • \(R=(Z_2-Z_1)/(Z_2+Z_1)\) with a stated polarity convention.

Misconception to surface

Saturation often raises shallow P-wave velocity across the unsaturated–saturated transition; its effect is not a universal “slower saturated zone,” and S waves respond differently.

Undergraduate Core
Fit branches, calculate intercepts, and recognize velocity inversion.
Graduate Extension
Compare reciprocal picks and inversion resolution; propagate pick uncertainty into interface depth.

Chapter 5 · Charge transport

Electrical, SP, and IP Methods

Separate rock-physics controls from array geometry and inversion, then compare galvanic resistivity with passive SP and time/frequency-dependent IP.

Deck basis: Electrical Methods, ERT Principles—Three-Layer Model, and Self-Potential & IP Methods.
1 · ConductionPore fluids, Archie, clay surface conduction, salinity, temperature.
2 · Survey responseGeometric factor, apparent resistivity, VES/ERT arrays, SP/IP observables.
3 · InversionSensitivity, regularization, equivalence, calibration, joint interpretation.
0–8 min · HookWhy can clay, saline water, and contamination all look conductive?
8–23 min · PhysicsCalculate Archie only after naming its clean-sand assumptions.
23–36 min · MeasurementBuild Wenner geometric factor and distinguish true from apparent resistivity.
36–47 min · VES teamsExplore H/A curves, suppression, equivalence, and DOI limits.
47–50 min · ExitWrite one sentence that does not overclaim water content.

Non-negotiable distinctions

  • Measured voltage/current → apparent resistivity → inverted model → geological interpretation.
  • SP and IP do not directly measure water content.
  • Cole–Cole exponent and Fourier-sign conventions must be declared.

Misconception to surface

A low resistivity cell is not uniquely clay, water, salinity, or contaminant; Archie conversion requires site calibration and often fails in clay-rich media.

Undergraduate Core
Calculate K and apparent resistivity, classify VES shape, and name ambiguity.
Graduate Extension
Compare sensitivity, regularization, equivalence, and petrophysical error propagation.

Chapter 6 · Inductive diffusion

Electromagnetic Methods

Trace the transmitter–earth–receiver chain in FDEM and TEM, using diffusion-depth calculations as teaching proxies rather than full forward responses.

Deck basis: EM Methods—Foundations and Deep EM Methods.
1 · InductionFaraday's law, primary field, eddy currents, secondary field.
2 · FDEM / TEMFrequency, quadrature/in-phase response, turn-off, decay gates.
3 · Survey choiceDiffusion, geometry, coupling, cultural noise, inversion.
0–8 min · HookFollow one transmitted field through a conductive plume.
8–23 min · PhysicsAnimate induction and distinguish diffusive response from wave reflection.
23–36 min · CompareMap frequency to FDEM sensitivity and time gate to TEM sensitivity.
36–47 min · Lab proxyChoose a plume frequency and compare two TEM sites.
47–50 min · ExitName what the skin-depth number does not include.

Equation / visual set

  • Skin depth as an attenuation scale, not a hard DOI.
  • Transmitter waveform, induced current system, and receiver datum.
  • System geometry and layered-earth sensitivity precede quantitative inversion.

Misconception to surface

Low-frequency fields in conductive earth are predominantly diffusive; they do not simply travel faster and reflect from a resistor like a seismic ray.

Undergraduate Core
Select FDEM/TEM settings using depth–resolution and noise trade-offs.
Graduate Extension
Replace the proxy with a system-specific layered-earth forward model and sensitivity analysis.

Chapter 7 · Radar propagation

Ground-Penetrating Radar

Use dielectric contrast, two-way time, antenna frequency, and acquisition geometry to make a conservative utility or shallow-stratigraphy interpretation.

Deck basis: Ground-Penetrating Radar—Integrated Course.
1 · PropagationPermittivity, conductivity, velocity, attenuation, wavelength.
2 · AcquisitionAntenna frequency, traces, profiles, time zero, positioning.
3 · ProcessingGain, filtering, velocity, migration, artifacts, depth uncertainty.
0–8 min · HookDecide whether an 18 ns utility is safe to cross with a 1.2 m auger.
8–22 min · PhysicsCompute velocity for dry and wet backfill and explain the difference.
22–35 min · RadargramRead horizons, hyperbolas, ringing, multiples, and attenuation.
35–47 min · Field planChoose 250 versus 1000 MHz for a target beneath clay-prone ground.
47–50 min · ExitState the velocity evidence needed before reporting depth.

Equation / visual set

  • \(v\approx c/\sqrt{\epsilon_r}\) under a low-loss approximation.
  • \(d=vt/2\): t is two-way time.
  • Reflection polarity requires an explicit field and display convention.

Misconception to surface

A bright hyperbola is not automatically a pipe, and time is not depth until velocity and time zero are constrained.

Undergraduate Core
Convert a plausible velocity range to a depth range and make a safe field recommendation.
Graduate Extension
Quantify hyperbola-fit, anisotropy, antenna-separation, migration, and zero-time uncertainty.

Chapter 8 · Natural-source EM

Magnetotellurics and Marine CSEM

Build MT from natural electric and magnetic time series to impedance, apparent resistivity, phase, dimensionality, and deep conductive-zone interpretation.

Deck basis: MT & Deep Crustal Methods and Deep EM Methods.
1 · Source & stationLightning/solar sources, Ex/Ey, Hx/Hy, remote reference.
2 · Data productsTensor impedance, apparent resistivity, phase, period sensitivity.
3 · Earth model1D/2D/3D, static shift, distortion, inversion, integration.
0–8 min · HookHow can a passive station sense from hundreds of meters to the mantle?
8–23 min · PhysicsForm E/H impedance and calculate the uniform half-space response.
23–35 min · WorkflowMove from time series through robust spectra to tensor data and errors.
35–47 min · InterpretationCompare a deep conductor with fluids, graphite, and melt hypotheses.
47–50 min · ExitExplain why phase heuristics are safest only in approximately 1D settings.

Equation / visual set

  • \(\mathbf{E}=\mathbf{Z}\mathbf{H}\), \(\rho_a=|Z|^2/(\mu_0\omega)\).
  • Uniform half-space phase is 45°, not a universal layered-earth rule.
  • Marine CSEM is a controlled, low-frequency diffusive-field method using amplitude and phase.

Misconception to surface

An MT inversion images electrical structure, not rock type. A conductor supports several geological explanations and retains strong depth–conductance trade-offs.

Undergraduate Core
Calculate apparent resistivity and explain period-dependent sensitivity.
Graduate Extension
Use tensor dimensionality, distortion tests, error floors, and model sensitivity before interpretation.

Chapter 9 · In-hole measurements

Borehole Geophysics

Organize logs by measured signal and investigation volume, then combine complementary responses while honoring casing, borehole, calibration, and resolution effects.

Deck basis: Borehole Geophysics—Principles and Applications and Advanced Technical Borehole Geophysics.
1 · Common logsGamma, resistivity/induction, sonic, density, neutron, caliper.
2 · Environmental toolsTeleviewer, NMR, fluorescence, temperature, flowmeter.
3 · Joint logDepth match, environmental corrections, calibration, hydrostratigraphy.
0–8 min · HookWhich logs can distinguish a clay aquitard from a water-bearing fracture?
8–24 min · Tool physicsMatch each tool to source, detector, measured signal, and sampling volume.
24–36 min · Log panelJointly read gamma, caliper, induction, neutron, televiewer, and flow.
36–47 min · Failure casesDiagnose casing, mud, invasion, washout, decentralization, and thin beds.
47–50 min · ExitName the calibration needed before converting one log to porosity or K.

Interpretation discipline

  • Measured count, voltage, travel time, or field response first.
  • Calibrated formation property second.
  • Lithology, fluid, fracture, or hydraulic interpretation only after joint evidence.

Misconception to surface

Neutron, NMR, induction, and natural gamma are not direct universal readouts of porosity, hydraulic conductivity, salinity, or clay content; tool response and calibration matter.

Undergraduate Core
Choose a defensible log suite and identify environmental corrections.
Graduate Extension
Model response-function mixing, vertical resolution, invasion, and uncertainty in joint petrophysical interpretation.
